fine_tuning
// Taking a general AI and teaching it to be an expert at one specific job
💡 The core idea: Think of a general-purpose AI like a college graduate who studied everything. Fine-tuning is like sending them to medical school — they already know how to think and communicate; now they just learn medicine specifically. You take an existing AI and train it further on your own specialized data.
Compare different fine-tuning approaches
Prompt Engineering
Add instructions to your prompt to guide the AI. No training needed — works with any AI via the API.
✓ Free — no training cost
✓ Instant — change anytime
✓ No data needed
✗ Takes up context space
✗ Inconsistent behavior
✗ Easy to "break" with clever inputs
LoRA Fine-tuning
Add a small set of "adapter" layers on top of the frozen model. Train only these (~1% of params). Very cheap.
✓ Very cheap to train
✓ Strong behavior change
✓ Easy to switch adapters
✗ Still needs 100s-1000s of examples
✗ Requires ML knowledge
Full Fine-tuning
Update every weight in the entire model on your data. Strongest specialization. Expensive.
✓ Maximum customization
✓ Deeply learns your domain
✗ Very expensive (GPUs × days)
✗ Risk of "catastrophic forgetting"
✗ Needs 10,000s+ examples
RAG (Retrieval)
Don't train at all — just give the AI relevant documents at query time. The AI reads the docs and answers based on them.
✓ No training cost
✓ Knowledge can be updated instantly
✓ Citations always available
✗ Slower (retrieval step)
✗ Limited by context window
When in doubt: try prompt engineering first → then RAG → then LoRA → full fine-tune as last resort
Step 0 / 5
When should you fine-tune?
Fine-tune when: (1) you need consistent behavior across many requests, (2) your domain is very specialized (medical, legal, coding in a niche language), (3) you need the AI to follow a very specific format or tone every time. Don't fine-tune when: the task can be done with a good prompt, you only have a few dozen examples, or you need real-time updated information (use RAG instead).
